===== Intro =====

Irrigation accounts for roughly 70% of global freshwater withdrawals and about 90% of consumptive freshwater use, making it one of the largest human interventions in the terrestrial water cycle (Siebert et al., 2010; McDermid et al., 2023). In the United States, approximately 22 million hectares of agricultural land are irrigated, and irrigation is the largest category of consumptive freshwater use nationally (USDA NASS, 2022; Maupin et al., 2014). Because irrigation withdrawals are large, spatially heterogeneous, and often contested, accurate accounting is essential for water management, drought response, and agricultural policy.

The most direct way to quantify crop and vegetation water use is to measure evapotranspiration (ET) in the field. Lysimeters, eddy covariance towers, and flux networks such as FLUXNET and AmeriFlux provide high-value observations and have greatly advanced understanding of land-atmosphere water exchange (Rana and Katerji, 2000; Baldocchi et al., 2001; Novick et al., 2018). However, these measurements are expensive, spatially sparse, and impractical for wall-to-wall operational accounting over irrigated regions, creating a clear need for scalable ET estimation methods.

Pre-satellite approaches to estimating ET at scale, including temperature-based empirical formulas and generalized crop schedules, estimated water requirements under assumed reference conditions rather than realized field ET, and their performance degraded substantially outside calibration regions (Jensen et al., 1990; Allen and Pruitt, 1986). These limitations established the need for spatially explicit, observation-driven ET estimation.

FAO-56 marked a major advance by standardizing crop ET estimation as a daily soil water balance driven by reference evapotranspiration and crop coefficients (Allen et al., 1998; Allen et al., 2005; Pereira et al., 2015). In that framework, atmospheric demand is represented by reference ET for a hypothetical, well-watered grass surface, while crop development and stress are expressed through crop and soil evaporation coefficients. FAO-56 and related soil water balance models remain widely used because they are physically interpretable, computationally efficient, and transferable across regions when driven by weather, soils, and generalized crop information (Allen, 2000; Pereira et al., 2015). Their central weakness is that tabulated crop-coefficient and phenology curves often require local tuning and still fail to represent actual planting dates, cultivar vigor, and stress timing at individual fields (Allen et al., 2001; Piccinni et al., 2009). That limitation motivated the shift toward vegetation-index-based crop coefficients and other remote-sensing approaches that replace fixed lookup tables with observed canopy condition (Hunsaker et al., 2003; Hunsaker et al., 2005; Glenn et al., 2011).

Satellite remote sensing created a new path for ET estimation by observing the land surface directly rather than inferring crop condition only from generalized schedules. Thermal infrared imagery is especially important because land surface temperature integrates moisture status and turbulent cooling, allowing ET to be estimated through the surface energy balance and, in two-source formulations, by partitioning soil and canopy fluxes separately (Norman et al., 1995; Bastiaanssen et al., 1998; Anderson et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2016). This physical basis established satellite ET as a mature scientific field rather than a simple empirical convenience. SEBAL solved latent heat as the residual of the surface energy balance using hot and cold anchor pixels (Bastiaanssen et al., 1998). METRIC adapted that paradigm for irrigated agriculture through internal calibration to local reference ET, improving local realism and supporting temporal interpolation through ET fractions (Allen et al., 2007). ALEXI and disALEXI extended the two-source framework by coupling land surface temperature change to atmospheric boundary layer growth and then disaggregating regional fluxes to Landsat scales, enabling daily ET estimation from fields to continents (Anderson et al., 1997; Anderson et al., 2004; Anderson et al., 2011). Together, these energy-balance models established the core strengths of thermal ET methods: physical grounding, applicability across crop types without explicit crop parameters, and direct sensitivity to water stress expressed in surface temperature.

Not all field-scale ET algorithms depend on full energy-balance closure. SIMS uses optical reflectance and crop-coefficient logic to estimate ET from observed canopy condition rather than direct thermal retrievals, operationalizing the vegetation-index crop-coefficient bridge between FAO-56 and satellite observations (Melton et al., 2012; Glenn et al., 2011). PT-JPL constrains potential ET with ecophysiological moisture scalars derived from remotely sensed vegetation and atmospheric demand, providing a parsimonious biophysical alternative that performs well across heterogeneous surfaces (Fisher et al., 2008). SSEBop takes a different path, expressing ET as reference ET scaled by a simplified thermal ET fraction tied to climatological hot and cold conditions, which makes the method computationally efficient for operational mapping (Senay et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2016). This algorithm diversity matters because no single model class is universally superior: thermal, reflectance-based, and simplified approaches capture complementary aspects of the ET process and respond differently to advection, cloud gaps, and land-cover heterogeneity (Gonzalez-Dugo et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2016).

At coarser resolution, global ET products established that remote sensing could support consistent water-use monitoring over long periods and broad domains. MOD16, GLEAM, and longer-record AVHRR-based systems made ET available for continental and global water-budget analyses, drought monitoring, and land-atmosphere studies (Mu et al., 2011; Martens et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2010). These products are valuable for large-scale assessment, but field-scale irrigation accounting requires approximately 10-30 m resolution, matching the scale of individual agricultural fields and management units. That spatial requirement explains why Landsat, and increasingly Sentinel-2 for crop-condition retrievals, became the critical sensors for operational agricultural ET mapping (Gowda et al., 2008; Senay et al., 2016; Rozenstein et al., 2019).

OpenET represented a major operational step change by implementing six leading Landsat-scale ET models in a common cloud-native framework on Google Earth Engine, making 30 m ET estimation practical across the Landsat archive for the western United States (Melton et al., 2022). Its importance is not only computational. OpenET created a shared platform for algorithm intercomparison, ensemble products, uncertainty characterization, and operational delivery to water agencies, irrigation districts, and water users. In croplands, the OpenET ensemble matched or exceeded individual models across most evaluation metrics, with mean seasonal ET within about 8% of flux tower totals in the framework paper and strong cropland accuracy in the larger CONUS assessment (Melton et al., 2022; Volk et al., 2024). More broadly, the operational ET ecosystem is moving toward denser revisit and complementary thermal sampling through planned Sentinel-2 integration and ECOSTRESS, increasing the observational infrastructure available for field-scale water accounting (Melton et al., 2022; Hulley et al., 2017; Xiao et al., 2021).

Despite their strength, satellite ET products still depend on clear-sky acquisitions and therefore require temporal interpolation to create daily, monthly, or annual ET fields. Those interpolation steps are necessary for tabulation over fields, irrigation districts, and political boundaries, but they mean that even highly accurate ET products estimate water use from atmospheric and surface state observations rather than from an explicit field water balance (Gowda et al., 2008; Melton et al., 2022). As a result, they do not directly enforce constraints imposed by precipitation inputs, soil water storage, groundwater subsidy, or irrigation supply. This is the key limitation for irrigation accounting: total ET can be mapped well while the relative contributions of irrigation and effective precipitation remain only weakly constrained, and irrigation-driven basin responses remain outside the scope of ET products alone (Gowda et al., 2008; Melton et al., 2022; Ketchum et al., 2023).

One response to this limitation has been to merge remote-sensing ET with soil water balance models. In the Upper Colorado River Basin, Pearson et al. (2024) combined FAO-56-style ET-Demands modeling with eeMETRIC satellite ET to estimate historical irrigated consumptive use, showing that hybrid remote-sensing plus process-model workflows can advance ET partitioning beyond ET products alone while still relying on regionally assigned crop coefficients. Related studies have used NDVI-derived crop coefficients or satellite ET to drive or calibrate FAO-56-type balances, but typically at one or two sites, with fixed Kc-NDVI relationships or inversion of only a single parameter (Er-Raki et al., 2007; Padilla et al., 2011; Campos et al., 2016). These studies show that remote sensing can improve process-model realism, but the soil water balance side of the framework often still inherits rigid crop-development assumptions or very limited parameter estimation. What remains missing is a field-scale framework that uses multi-algorithm satellite ETf from a unified operational platform as ensemble calibration constraints, tests the approach across multiple crop types and irrigation regimes, and quantifies how algorithm choice propagates into posterior water-balance uncertainty — and a calibrated forward model can simulate daily fluxes through periods when optical and thermal imagery are unavailable, which image-based interpolation cannot.

This study addresses that gap by introducing SWIM-RS, a forward field-scale soil water balance framework calibrated against satellite ETf observations from multiple operational ET algorithms. Rather than adding another ET algorithm to an already mature remote-sensing literature, SWIM-RS aims to retain the phenological and field-scale fidelity of leading ET products while adding explicit water-balance constraints, continuous daily model output, and modeled estimates of consumptive use. The objectives are to: (1) develop and evaluate a framework that treats satellite-derived ETf from six OpenET algorithms as Bayesian ensemble calibration targets, integrating observations from Landsat, Sentinel-2, and ECOSTRESS; (2) validate simulated daily and monthly ET against an independent network of more than 150 flux tower sites spanning diverse crop types, land-cover classes, and irrigation settings, benchmarked against SSEBop NHM on the same valid observations; (3) assess whether multi-algorithm ensemble ETf calibration improves ET simulation skill relative to the Volk et al. (2024) operational multi-model ensemble product; and (4) demonstrate the portability of the framework to an international cropland cohort spanning five continents using globally available inputs — ERA5-Land meteorology, HWSD soils, and a two-model Landsat ETf ensemble (SSEBop + PT-JPL) as the international calibration target. These objectives are evaluated through three experiments with a progressive logic — demonstrate, optimize, generalize: a multi-land-cover CONUS demonstration calibrated against single-algorithm SSEBop NHM ETf (Experiment 4), a cropland-focused optimization that calibrates against a weighted ensemble of six OpenET algorithms (Experiment 5), and an international generalization to globally available ERA5-Land and HWSD inputs calibrated against the per-overpass mean of Landsat SSEBop and PT-JPL ETf (Experiment 6). As a first evaluation of SWIM-RS, this study focuses on ET because ET is the dominant outgoing hydrologic flux in irrigated regions; validation of simulated runoff and deep percolation is deferred to future work.

===== Study Area =====

The three experiments draw on distinct flux-tower cohorts, described below. Each site is represented by a polygon — either a three-point footprint geometry or a 150 m buffer around the tower location — that defines the field-scale analysis unit for all remote sensing extractions and model simulations.

Experiment 4 encompasses 161 sites across the contiguous United States spanning six land-cover classes: cropland (60), grassland (30), shrubland (29), evergreen forest (19), mixed forest (14), and wetland/riparian (9). Sites span the major climate and agricultural regions of CONUS, from the humid Southeast and Corn Belt to the arid interior West.

Experiment 5 retains the 60 cropland sites from Experiment 4 as a focused subset for evaluating multi-algorithm ensemble calibration on the land-cover class most relevant to irrigation accounting.

Experiment 6 extends the analysis internationally, with 75 cropland and pasture/grassland sites distributed across 14 countries spanning five continents (North America, Europe, Asia, Oceania, and South America). International sites were drawn from AmeriFlux, FLUXNET, ICOS, and OzFlux networks and filtered for cropland or managed-pasture land cover with post-2013 flux tower data coverage coincident with the Landsat 8/9 OLI archive.

===== Methods =====

=== Experimental Design ===

SWIM-RS was evaluated across three applications that share a common modeling framework — the soil water balance, NDVI-driven crop coefficient, PEST++ IES calibration procedure, and flux-tower validation protocol described below. Each experiment tests that framework against a different satellite ETf calibration target, a different period of record, and a different combination of meteorological and soil inputs. Each site is calibrated independently. No flux tower data are used to configure or constrain model inputs; towers serve exclusively as validation targets. All three experiments use unmasked site footprints with no irrigation or land-cover spatial filtering applied to remote sensing extractions.

**Experiment 4** calibrates against USGS SSEBop NHM Landsat ETf (Senay et al., 2013; 2023) over 1987–2025, using GridMET meteorology and SSURGO soils.

**Experiment 5** calibrates against the unweighted mean of six OpenET Landsat ETf models — SSEBop, PT-JPL, SIMS, geeSEBAL, eeMETRIC, and DisALEXI (Melton et al., 2022) — over 1995–2025, with per-observation weights inversely proportional to inter-model spread (see Calibration).

**Experiment 6** calibrates against the per-overpass mean of Landsat SSEBop and Landsat PT-JPL ETf over 2013–2025, using ERA5-Land meteorology and HWSD soils. SSEBop ETf is delivered through the USGS ESPA on-demand processing system (Senay et al., 2013; 2023), while PT-JPL ETf is extracted via Google Earth Engine using the openet-ptjpl package (Fisher et al., 2008; Melton et al., 2022). Averaging the two models on each overpass date exploits their opposing biases — SSEBop underestimates and PT-JPL overestimates relative to flux tower ET — to produce a calibration target with near-zero aggregate bias, following the same ensemble logic as Experiment 5 but limited to the two models available at global scale.

=== Remote Sensing Inputs ===

Remote sensing inputs serve two distinct roles in SWIM-RS: optical imagery from Landsat and Sentinel-2 provides the NDVI time series that drives the sigmoid crop coefficient, while thermal-based ET algorithms applied to Landsat imagery provide the ETf calibration targets.

We tracked vegetation phenology through field-level zonal statistics extracted from harmonized Landsat and Sentinel-2 surface reflectance imagery on Google Earth Engine. Because the Landsat archive spans five sensors with different spectral response functions, all reflectances are harmonized to the Landsat 8 OLI reference before computing NDVI. Landsat 4/5 TM and Landsat 7 ETM+ Red and NIR bands are transformed to OLI-equivalent reflectance using the ordinary least squares coefficients of Roy et al. (2016), and Sentinel-2 MSI Red and narrow-NIR (B8A) bands are adjusted using the spectral bandpass adjustment factors from the Harmonized Landsat and Sentinel-2 (HLS) product (Claverie et al., 2018). These linear transformations are applied at the image level during extraction, yielding a spectrally consistent fused NDVI time series across all sensors with approximately weekly effective revisit during cloud-free periods. Each NDVI observation represents the spatial mean within the site polygon.

The primary calibration targets are ET fraction values retrieved from satellite-based ET algorithms at each clear-sky overpass:

Eq. 1: ETf = ETa / ETo

where ETa is actual evapotranspiration (mm d⁻¹) and ETo is grass-reference evapotranspiration (mm d⁻¹). This normalization isolates the crop and soil moisture signal from day-to-day atmospheric demand variation, making ETf a dimensionless index that is more transferable across climates and instruments than absolute ET (Senay et al., 2013).

For Experiments 4 and 5, ETf is extracted from Landsat-based algorithms implemented on Google Earth Engine via the openet-* Python packages. Experiment 4 uses a single product — USGS SSEBop NHM (Senay et al., 2013; Senay et al., 2023). Experiment 5 uses the mean of six OpenET models: SSEBop, PT-JPL, SIMS, geeSEBAL, eeMETRIC, and DisALEXI (Melton et al., 2022). The SSEBop ET fraction is derived from a simplified surface energy balance that scales between climatological hot and cold boundary conditions using land surface temperature (Senay et al., 2013). OpenET provides a standardized multi-model infrastructure for generating field-scale ET at 30 m resolution across the Landsat archive (Melton et al., 2022; Volk et al., 2024).

For Experiment 6, the calibration target is the per-overpass mean of two Landsat ETf products. SSEBop ETf is extracted as field-scale zonal means from per-scene Landsat Collection 2 SSEBop outputs ordered through the USGS ESPA on-demand processing system (Senay et al., 2013; 2023). PT-JPL ETf is extracted via Google Earth Engine using the openet-ptjpl package (Fisher et al., 2008; Melton et al., 2022). On each overpass date, the calibration target is the arithmetic mean of the two models; dates where only one model is available use the single available value.

=== Meteorological Forcing ===

Daily meteorological forcing for the CONUS experiments is drawn from GridMET (Abatzoglou, 2013), a 4-km resolution gridded dataset that combines PRISM monthly climatologies with NLDAS-2 reanalysis temporal patterns. Variables extracted include maximum and minimum temperature, precipitation, downward shortwave radiation, humidity, and wind speed. Reference evapotranspiration (ETo) is computed as ASCE standardized short-reference (grass) ET (Allen et al., 2005) from these inputs. We applied a static monthly bias correction to ETo using pre-computed correction factors derived from comparisons with weather station networks.

For the international experiment, daily meteorological forcing is drawn from ERA5-Land (Muñoz-Sabater et al., 2021), which provides hourly land surface variables at approximately 9 km global resolution by running the ECMWF land surface model forced by downscaled ERA5 atmospheric fields (Hersbach et al., 2020). We aggregated hourly fields to daily values and computed ETo from the same ASCE formulation. Because ERA5-Land radiation fields can carry systematic biases relative to ground observations, we derived monthly ETo correction factors from comparisons with nearby ISD and MADIS weather stations for North American sites within MADIS coverage; sites outside MADIS coverage use uncorrected ERA5-Land ETo.

=== Soil Properties ===

Available water capacity (AWC) — the difference between field capacity and permanent wilting point integrated over the rooting depth — is the primary soil input to the water balance. AWC defines the total water storage accessible to plants and directly controls the partitioning between ET, drainage, and runoff.

For CONUS sites (Experiments 4 and 5), we extracted profile-averaged AWC from the Soil Survey Geographic (SSURGO) database (Soil Survey Staff, NRCS), the most detailed soil survey available for the United States, with map unit delineations at scales from 1:12,000 to 1:63,360. Horizon-level AWC values are depth-weighted across the soil profile for each site polygon.

For international sites (Experiment 6), we extracted AWC from the Harmonized World Soil Database v1.2 (Nachtergaele et al., 2008), a 30 arc-second global raster that provides topsoil (0–30 cm) and subsoil (30–100 cm) attributes including available water storage capacity. HWSD provides globally consistent soil information at lower spatial resolution than SSURGO but with sufficient coverage for the international network.

In both cases, the extracted AWC serves as the prior central estimate for calibration; the actual AWC used in simulation is adjusted by PEST++ IES within bounds of 100–400 mm based on the fit to satellite ETf observations.

We assigned maximum rooting depth from a MODIS IGBP land-cover lookup table following Yang et al. (2016); rooting depth was not calibrated.

=== Irrigation Status ===

Irrigation status — whether a site is classified as irrigated or rainfed — controls several model behaviors: it determines prior parameter bounds (e.g., a narrower management allowed depletion range for irrigated fields), activates or deactivates the irrigation demand routine, and affects the interpretation of calibrated water-balance partitioning. For CONUS sites in Experiments 4 and 5, we determined irrigation status from two Landsat-derived annual irrigation maps. In the western U.S., IrrMapper (Ketchum et al., 2020) provides 30-m annual classifications from 1986 to 2018 using a Random Forest classifier trained on over 50,000 human-verified irrigated fields, with binary accuracy of 97.8%. In the eastern U.S. where IrrMapper is unavailable, LANID (Xie and Lark, 2019; Xie and Lark, 2021) extends 30-m annual irrigation mapping across CONUS from 1997 to 2017 with overall accuracy above 90%. We classified a site as irrigated in a given year if the majority of the site polygon was mapped as irrigated by the applicable product.

For international sites in Experiment 6, where neither IrrMapper nor LANID is available, we inferred irrigation status from a combination of MODIS IGBP land-cover class and a water-balance criterion: cropland sites where mean annual ET exceeds mean annual precipitation are classified as irrigated. This heuristic captures the defining characteristic of irrigated agriculture — consumptive use in excess of local rainfall — without requiring a U.S.-specific mapping product.

=== Soil Water Balance Model ===

SWIM-RS implements a daily FAO-56 dual crop coefficient soil water balance (Allen et al., 1998). Each day, incoming precipitation is partitioned into rain and snow by a temperature threshold; snowpack melts via a calibrated degree-day function (melt = swe_alpha + swe_beta × T_max). Rain and snowmelt enter the SCS Curve Number runoff routine, which removes surface runoff based on antecedent soil moisture before water infiltrates the soil profile.

The central departure from standard FAO-56 practice is the crop coefficient. Rather than tabulated phenology schedules, SWIM-RS computes the basal crop coefficient (Kcb) from NDVI through a sigmoid transfer function:

Eq. 2: Kcb = Kc_max / (1 + exp(−ndvi_k × (NDVI − ndvi_0)))

where Kc_max is the maximum crop coefficient, ndvi_k controls the steepness of the response, and ndvi_0 is the NDVI inflection point. Between satellite overpass dates, NDVI is linearly interpolated from a fused Landsat–Sentinel-2 time series so that the model tracks actual field-level vegetation development — late plantings, failed crops, double-cropping, perennial canopies — rather than a regional average. The sigmoid form is motivated by the well-documented nonlinearity between NDVI, leaf area index, and canopy light interception: NDVI saturates at moderate-to-high LAI while transpiration continues to increase, so a linear NDVI-Kcb mapping systematically underestimates crop coefficients during peak growth and overestimates them during early growth when soil background inflates NDVI (Carlson and Ripley, 1997; Duchemin et al., 2006; Nguy-Robertson et al., 2012). The sigmoid captures these saturation effects through its asymptotic structure, while the calibrated inflection point and steepness accommodate crop-specific and site-specific variation in the NDVI-Kcb relationship (Pocas et al., 2020).

Daily ET is then computed following the FAO-56 dual coefficient formulation (Allen et al., 1998):

Eq. 3: ETa = (Kcb × Ks + Ke) × ETo

where Ks is a water stress coefficient that reduces transpiration linearly as root-zone depletion exceeds a readily-available-water threshold, governed by total available water (TAW) and a management allowed depletion fraction (Allen et al., 1998). Ke is a soil evaporation coefficient from the FAO-56 two-stage model. Calibrated damping parameters (ks_alpha, kr_alpha) control the rate at which stress and evaporation reduction intensify with depletion. For irrigated fields, irrigation is triggered when root-zone depletion exceeds the management allowed depletion threshold, refilling the profile to field capacity. Water in excess of field capacity drains as deep percolation through a three-layer storage scheme (evaporative layer, root zone, sub-root reservoir).

The model's ET fraction on each day (Eq. 1) provides the simulated counterpart to the satellite-observed calibration targets.

=== Calibration ===

Parameter estimation uses pestpp-ies (White, 2018), a model-independent implementation of the Levenberg-Marquardt iterative ensemble smoother (Chen and Oliver, 2013). The algorithm updates an ensemble of parameter realizations by iteratively minimizing a regularized objective function that balances data misfit against deviation from the prior distribution, using ensemble-derived cross-covariance between parameters and observations in place of an explicit Jacobian matrix. This approach provides both optimized parameter estimates (from the ensemble median) and posterior uncertainty (from the ensemble spread) without requiring adjoint model derivatives (Emerick and Reynolds, 2013; White et al., 2020).

Each site is calibrated independently. The ensemble consists of 200 parameter realizations drawn from the prior distribution, updated over three IES iterations. SWIM-RS operates as a black-box forward model: pestpp-ies writes candidate parameter sets, invokes the forward model to produce simulated ETf time series, compares them to satellite-observed ETf, and updates the ensemble accordingly. The pyemu Python package (White et al., 2016) constructs the PEST control files, parameter bounds, observation groups, prior parameter covariance, and template/instruction file interface.

Eight parameters are calibrated per site (Table S1): the three sigmoid coefficients (Kc_max, ndvi_k, ndvi_0), available water capacity, water stress and evaporation damping coefficients (ks_alpha, kr_alpha), management allowed depletion, and two snowmelt degree-day coefficients. Prior distributions are Gaussian, centered on physically motivated defaults with standard deviations reflecting expected inter-site variability.

The objective function minimized by pestpp-ies is the sum of squared weighted residuals between simulated and observed ETf at each satellite overpass date:

Eq. 4: Φ = Σᵢ wᵢ (ETf_sim,i − ETf_obs,i)²

where the sum is over all satellite overpass dates i, ETf_sim,i and ETf_obs,i are the simulated and observed ET fractions, and wᵢ is the per-observation weight. Experiments 4 and 6 calibrate against a single ETf algorithm, so all observations receive a fixed weight wᵢ = ETf_obs,i / 0.33. Experiment 5, which calibrates against a multi-model ensemble, enables a richer weighting scheme:

Eq. 5: wᵢ = ETf_obs,i / (σ_ensemble,i + 0.1)

where σ_ensemble,i is the inter-model standard deviation across the six OpenET algorithms at overpass date i. Dates with strong algorithm agreement receive higher weight, so that calibration is guided more by observations where the retrieval is most reliable. A floor of 0.1 prevents singularity; only dates with at least two ensemble members are eligible. This spread-based weighting is a benefit of ensemble calibration beyond the accuracy gain from averaging multiple algorithms — the inter-model spread carries per-observation information about retrieval reliability that a single-algorithm target cannot provide. The performance gain from spread-based versus fixed-SD weighting is evaluated in the Results.

=== Validation Dataset ===

Independent validation uses daily ET measurements from eddy covariance flux towers. No flux tower data are used to configure or constrain model inputs; towers serve exclusively as validation targets. For Experiments 4 and 5, the flux tower dataset is the CONUS OpenET flux network compiled and benchmarked by Volk et al. (2024), which we retain as the authoritative reference for U.S. flux evaluation. For Experiment 6, where the Volk benchmark does not extend, we use a multi-network archive assembled specifically to support international ET benchmarking. The archive aggregates AmeriFlux, FLUXNET2015 (Pastorello et al., 2020), ICOS, and OzFlux into a single inventory (832 daily site-files, 704 unique site IDs) with manifest-driven resolution of sites that appear in multiple networks. Each site is reprocessed from raw half-hourly or hourly records through the flux-data-qaqc package (Volk et al., 2021) — the same open-source implementation used to produce the AgForMet cropland benchmark — under a strict default of energy-balance-residual closure correction and no ET gap-filling, so every validation day represents an observed, closure-corrected flux rather than an ETrF-interpolated reconstruction.

Eddy covariance systems systematically underestimate latent and sensible heat fluxes due to energy balance non-closure, with turbulent fluxes typically accounting for only 80–85% of available energy across flux networks (Wilson et al., 2002; Stoy et al., 2013). Uncorrected tower ET would therefore create a systematic low bias in the validation reference. To address this, we corrected all flux tower ET values for energy balance closure using the Bowen ratio energy balance residual method (Twine et al., 2000; Wilson et al., 2002; Volk et al., 2021):

Eq. 6: LE_corr = LE × (Rn − G) / (LE + H)

where Rn is net radiation, G is ground heat flux, and LE and H are the measured latent and sensible heat fluxes. The correction scales turbulent fluxes by the energy balance ratio so that their sum equals available energy while preserving the observed Bowen ratio. ET_corr is derived from LE_corr via the latent heat of vaporization and is used as the validation reference throughout; sites whose energy-balance set (Rn, G, LE, H) is incomplete fall to a raw-ET auxiliary tier and are reported separately rather than silently substituted.

For Experiment 6 the international archive is further restricted to cropland and agriculturally-managed sites using a curated cohort of 75 sites (49 United States and 21 non-U.S. distributed across 14 countries on five continents) selected from a broader 96-site pool by requiring post-2013 flux tower data coincident with Landsat OLI coverage. All sites carry a final cropland land-cover class from the FROM-GLC10 10 m product, including sites whose raster values were manually overridden from network IGBP class and site-team documentation to correct misclassifications at the 10 m scale (rainfed cropland misclassified as grassland or impervious, documented irrigated cropland, and intensively managed pasture).

We assessed model performance using a paired evaluation design: SWIM-RS and the satellite benchmark are scored on the identical set of valid days or months at each site, so that neither model benefits from a larger or more favorable sample. In Experiment 4, the benchmark is SSEBop ET computed from interpolated SSEBop ETf multiplied by daily ETo. In Experiment 5, the benchmark is the Volk et al. (2024) 3x3 OpenET daily ET extractions, which provide daily ET (mm/day) from the same six models and their ensemble mean via a separate extraction and aggregation pipeline. The Volk benchmark shares model ancestry with the ensemble-mean ETf calibration target but is a distinct product — daily ET aggregated over a 3x3 Landsat pixel window, not the dimensionless ETf values at individual overpass dates used to fit parameters. Both SWIM-RS and the Volk benchmark are scored against the same fully independent reference (flux tower ET), so the comparison tests whether the calibrated process model can match or exceed the satellite ensemble's accuracy when evaluated on independent observations. We computed monthly performance only for site-months with at least 20 valid paired daily observations; sites with fewer than three qualifying months are excluded from the monthly evaluation. Performance metrics include coefficient of determination (R-squared), root mean square error (RMSE), and mean bias error (MBE) computed at both daily and monthly time steps.

===== Results =====

=== Calibration Behavior and Parameter Posteriors ===

The PEST++ IES objective function (Phi) decreased rapidly for both experiments, falling approximately 80 percent from the prior ensemble by the first iteration and stabilizing by the third. The convergence trajectory was smooth in both cases, indicating that the ensemble smoother found consistent parameter updates rather than oscillating between competing optima.

Posterior parameter distributions varied systematically across land cover classes in ways consistent with known plant-water dynamics. Cropland sites converged to high available water capacity (median approximately 300 mm), reflecting the deep, well-structured agricultural soils typical of flux tower cropland sites. Shrubland and grassland sites settled at lower AWC values (medians near 200 mm), consistent with shallower or coarser soils in arid and semi-arid settings. The NDVI inflection point (ndvi_0) separated cleanly: cropland posteriors clustered near 0.55, while shrubland and grassland posteriors centered near 0.25, reflecting the lower peak NDVI at which these canopies reach maximum transpiration rates. Management allowed depletion was tighter for croplands (median approximately 0.35) than for natural vegetation classes (0.40-0.50), consistent with the managed irrigation scheduling at cropland sites. These land-cover-dependent shifts were not prescribed — all sites shared the same prior distributions — but emerged from the calibration data, suggesting that the inversion recovers physically interpretable parameter structure rather than arbitrary curve fits.

=== Example 4: Multi-Land-Cover Evaluation ===

The Experiment 4 evaluation covers 317,351 paired daily observations across 159 sites, with a median of 1,227 paired days per site. Two sites were excluded for data quality, leaving 159 in the final analysis.

At the daily timescale, SWIM-RS achieved a median R-squared of 0.46 compared to 0.40 for SSEBop. SWIM-RS outperformed SSEBop at 70 percent of sites (112 of 159). Median RMSE was 0.98 mm d⁻¹ for SWIM-RS versus 1.09 mm d⁻¹ for SSEBop. SWIM-RS was nearly unbiased at the network level, with a median bias of +0.01 mm d⁻¹, while SSEBop showed a systematic dry bias of -0.16 mm d⁻¹. Seventy-five percent of sites had positive SWIM-RS R-squared values (120 of 159), compared to 72 percent for SSEBop (115 of 159).

At the monthly timescale, the advantage persisted. SWIM-RS achieved a median R-squared of 0.61 versus 0.51 for SSEBop, outperforming at 67 percent of sites (96 of 143). Median RMSE was 20.8 mm mo⁻¹ for SWIM-RS versus 24.8 mm mo⁻¹ for SSEBop. SWIM-RS remained nearly unbiased (median -0.6 mm mo⁻¹), while SSEBop carried a dry bias of -3.9 mm mo⁻¹. Eighty percent of sites had positive SWIM-RS monthly R-squared (114 of 143), compared to 73 percent for SSEBop (105 of 143).

Performance varied substantially by land cover class (Table 1).

Three patterns stand out. First, absolute skill tracked vegetation complexity: croplands and evergreen forests — land covers with strong, predictable seasonal NDVI cycles — produced the highest R-squared for both models, while shrublands and grasslands, where low and variable NDVI makes the crop coefficient signal harder to resolve, had lower skill. Second, SWIM-RS won at a majority of sites in every land cover class, including grasslands (61 percent daily) where SSEBop had the higher median R-squared. The grassland median was pulled down by a small number of SWIM-RS outlier sites with strongly negative R-squared; SWIM-RS was the better model more often across the cohort. Third, bias patterns were class-dependent: SSEBop carried a persistent dry bias in croplands (median -0.38 mm d⁻¹ vs. +0.09 for SWIM-RS), consistent with the known tendency of simplified thermal methods to underestimate peak-season ET under advective conditions, while both models showed a positive bias of approximately 0.4 mm d⁻¹ at evergreen forest sites, likely reflecting energy balance closure problems at forested flux towers rather than model error.

Wetland and riparian sites were a structural limitation: both models produced negative median R-squared at daily and monthly timescales. The soil water balance cannot represent groundwater-subsidized ET because precipitation-driven water inputs are insufficient to explain the observed flux. This structural limitation — shared between SWIM-RS and SSEBop — motivates the exclusion of open-water and saturated wetland sites from the framework's intended operational scope.

=== Representative Time Series ===

Daily ET time series at four representative Experiment 4 sites illustrate the character of the SWIM-RS fit across land cover classes. At US-Ne1, an irrigated maize site in Nebraska (2007-2013), SWIM-RS closely tracked the seasonal ET cycle, matching both the sharp spring ramp-up driven by planting and the late-season decline. SSEBop captured the same seasonal shape but consistently underestimated peak-season ET. At US-ARM, a grassland site in Oklahoma, both models reproduced the annual cycle but SWIM-RS better matched the lower-amplitude seasonal signal and avoided the SSEBop tendency to overestimate winter ET. At US-NC3, a managed loblolly pine plantation in North Carolina (2014-2019), SWIM-RS tracked the high, sustained growing-season ET characteristic of evergreen forests, where SSEBop also performed well but with slightly more scatter. At US-CMW, a wetland/riparian site in Arizona, neither model reproduced the observed ET. The flux tower recorded sustained high ET through the growing season, but the soil water balance, receiving only precipitation inputs, could not maintain the necessary soil moisture. This site illustrates the structural limitation identified in the network-level statistics: without representing lateral inflows or shallow groundwater, the model cannot simulate wetland ET regardless of parameter calibration.

=== Example 5: Ensemble Calibration on Croplands ===

Experiment 5 narrows the scope to 58 cropland sites and replaces the single-model calibration target with the mean ETf of a six-model OpenET ensemble. The evaluation comprises 81,393 paired daily observations, with a median of 1,064 paired days per site. The validation benchmark is the Volk et al. (2024) 3x3 OpenET daily ET extraction. The Volk benchmark shares model ancestry with the ensemble-mean ETf used for calibration but is a distinct product — daily ET from a separate 3x3 pixel extraction pipeline, not the dimensionless overpass-date ETf values used to fit parameters. Both SWIM-RS and the Volk benchmark are evaluated against the same fully independent reference: energy-balance-corrected flux tower ET.

At the daily timescale, SWIM-RS achieved a median R-squared of 0.65 compared to 0.57 for the OpenET ensemble. SWIM-RS outperformed the ensemble at 71 percent of sites (41 of 58). Median RMSE was 1.14 mm d⁻¹ for SWIM-RS versus 1.19 mm d⁻¹ for the ensemble. Both models had small bias: SWIM-RS median bias was -0.01 mm d⁻¹ and the ensemble median bias was -0.09 mm d⁻¹. Ninety percent of sites had positive SWIM-RS R-squared (52 of 58), compared to 78 percent for the ensemble (45 of 58).

=== Overpass versus Non-Overpass Performance ===

Non-overpass days constituted roughly 95 percent of the record at typical Landsat revisit frequencies, so the process model's advantage on interpolated days dominated the overall daily statistics. Splitting the daily evaluation into Landsat overpass days and non-overpass days revealed where the process model added value. On overpass days (45 sites with at least 10 paired observations), the OpenET ensemble outperformed SWIM-RS: median R-squared of 0.75 versus 0.68, with the ensemble winning at 69 percent of sites. This was expected — on days with a direct satellite observation, the ensemble had a fresh retrieval while SWIM-RS relied on its process model. On non-overpass days (58 sites), the result reversed. SWIM-RS achieved a median R-squared of 0.65 versus 0.55 for the ensemble, winning at 74 percent of sites. SWIM-RS also had lower RMSE (1.13 versus 1.20 mm d⁻¹) and was nearly unbiased (median -0.001 mm d⁻¹) while the ensemble carried a small dry bias (-0.08 mm d⁻¹). The soil water balance provided physical continuity between satellite acquisitions — tracking precipitation events, soil moisture drawdown, and stress onset — that simple temporal interpolation of ETf could not reproduce.

=== Individual Model Comparisons ===

The daily advantage over individual OpenET models was larger still. SWIM-RS outperformed SSEBop (median R-squared 0.38) at 88 percent of sites, geeSEBAL (0.43) at 88 percent, eeMETRIC (0.35) at 88 percent, DisALEXI (0.45) at 84 percent, PT-JPL (0.55) at 81 percent, and SIMS (0.51) at 79 percent. The ensemble mean was the strongest single comparator, outperforming every individual model, but SWIM-RS still beat it at nearly three quarters of sites.

At the monthly timescale, the two approaches converged. SWIM-RS achieved a median R-squared of 0.85 versus 0.86 for the ensemble, winning at 52 percent of sites (17 of 33). The ensemble had slightly lower RMSE (19.6 mm mo⁻¹ versus 21.5 for SWIM-RS). However, SWIM-RS was dramatically less biased: median bias of +0.3 mm mo⁻¹ compared to -6.7 mm mo⁻¹ for the ensemble. All 58 sites with sufficient monthly data had positive SWIM-RS R-squared (33 of 33), versus 97 percent for the ensemble (32 of 33). The monthly convergence was consistent with the Experiment 4 finding that temporal aggregation narrows the gap between a calibrated process model and direct satellite retrievals.

Against individual models at the monthly timescale, SWIM-RS still held a clear advantage over SSEBop (wins 88 percent), geeSEBAL (91 percent), and eeMETRIC (82 percent). The margin narrowed against PT-JPL (76 percent) and was smallest against DisALEXI and SIMS (both 64 percent), the two most competitive individual models at longer aggregation windows.

=== Seasonal Totals and Annual Bias ===

For water management applications, seasonal and annual ET totals matter more than daily correlation. Cumulative growing-season (April through October) ET curves at three irrigated Experiment 5 cropland sites showed that SWIM-RS tracked the observed seasonal accumulation closely. At US-Ne1 in 2010, SWIM-RS accumulated 745 mm over the growing season compared to 813 mm measured at the flux tower, an underestimate of 8 percent. The OpenET ensemble accumulated only 643 mm, underestimating by 21 percent. At US-Tw3, a rice paddy in California in 2017, SWIM-RS accumulated 755 mm versus 718 mm observed (5 percent overestimate), while the ensemble reached only 309 mm — less than half the observed total — likely reflecting the difficulty that thermal-based ET models have with flooded rice paddies, where standing water alters emissivity and the relationship between land surface temperature and moisture status. At RIP760, an irrigated cropland in Idaho in 2018, SWIM-RS accumulated 986 mm versus 867 mm observed (14 percent overestimate), while the ensemble reached 869 mm (near-zero bias at that site).

Across the 33-site matched cropland cohort, the median annual bias was +36 mm yr⁻¹ for SWIM-RS and -16 mm yr⁻¹ for the OpenET ensemble. SWIM-RS carried a modest wet bias while the ensemble carried a modest dry bias, but the SWIM-RS bias distribution was more symmetric and had fewer extreme outliers. The practical implication is that SWIM-RS seasonal totals were competitive with the satellite ensemble for cumulative water-use accounting, while providing the daily time step and water-balance partitioning that satellite products alone could not deliver.

=== Spread-Based Observation Weighting ===

An ablation experiment tested whether spread-based observation weighting improved calibration relative to fixed-SD weighting. Both runs used the same sites, ensemble mean target, and PEST++ configuration; only the per-observation weights differed. Spread-based weighting produced a clear improvement at the daily timescale: median R-squared of 0.67 versus 0.59 for fixed-SD, with RMSE of 1.12 versus 1.17 mm d⁻¹. The spread-weighted run won at 59 percent of sites. At the monthly timescale the median R-squared advantage persisted (0.82 versus 0.77), but the win rate dropped to 47 percent, meaning the spread-weighted run won by larger margins at the sites it won but lost more often elsewhere. Both runs had small bias and identical runtime. Spread-based weighting improved daily ET simulation skill (median R-squared 0.67 vs. 0.59; RMSE 1.12 vs. 1.17 mm d⁻¹) relative to fixed-SD weighting. Monthly performance was comparable between schemes, suggesting that the weighting mechanism primarily benefits the daily calibration signal rather than accumulated totals.

=== Experiment 6: International Generalization ===

Experiment 6 tests the portability of the SWIM-RS framework to globally available inputs — ERA5-Land meteorology, HWSD soils, and a two-model Landsat ETf ensemble (SSEBop + PT-JPL) — across 75 cropland sites spanning 14 countries on five continents. The calibration period is 2013–2025, covering the Landsat 8/9 OLI era. Of the 75 calibrated sites, 70 had sufficient flux tower data for validation (at least 30 paired daily observations with closure-corrected ET).

The PEST++ IES calibration converged efficiently, with phi reductions of 74–76 percent across two batches (50 + 25 sites). All 2,800 model evaluations completed without failure.

At the daily timescale, the calibrated model achieved a median bias of −0.055 mm d⁻¹, MAE of 0.687 mm d⁻¹, RMSE of 0.930 mm d⁻¹, R-squared of 0.627, and KGE of 0.701 across the 70 validated sites. Fifty-one percent of sites had KGE above 0.7, and 79 percent exceeded 0.5. Compared to the uncalibrated LULC-defaults baseline on 30 common sites, calibration reduced bias from +0.32 to −0.05 mm d⁻¹ (84 percent reduction), RMSE from 1.19 to 0.96 mm d⁻¹ (19 percent), and nearly doubled R-squared from 0.36 to 0.61.

To contextualize SWIM-RS performance against the satellite products used as calibration targets, we compared daily ET from the calibrated model against linearly interpolated Landsat SSEBop and PT-JPL ET (ETf interpolated between overpass dates, multiplied by daily ETo). The comparison is conservative for SWIM-RS: the interpolated Landsat products are scored only on their valid-flux overpass days, while SWIM-RS is scored on all valid-flux days including the inter-overpass periods where no satellite observation is available.

A Murphy (1988) decomposition of mean squared error into bias, variance, and phase (correlation) components revealed that all three products were phase-dominated: 67–80 percent of MSE arose from correlation error — the irreducible timing mismatch inherent to reconstructing daily ET from 16-day satellite revisit — rather than from bias or variance mismatch. SWIM-RS and PT-JPL achieved comparable correlation (median r = 0.848 and 0.838, respectively), confirming that the calibrated process model did not improve daily timing relative to interpolated satellite ETf. Instead, SWIM-RS gained its advantage through bias correction and, to a lesser degree, through reduced variance error on inter-overpass days where the soil water balance tracked precipitation-driven ET dynamics that linear interpolation missed.

The CONUS and ex-CONUS subsets showed similar overall performance, with one notable structural difference. At CONUS sites (49 validated), SWIM-RS achieved a median KGE of 0.719, with the variability ratio (alpha = sigma_mod / sigma_obs) at 0.880 and the bias ratio (beta = mu_mod / mu_obs) at 0.964. At ex-CONUS sites (21 validated), median KGE was 0.670, with a near-perfect bias ratio (beta = 0.983) but a lower variability ratio (alpha = 0.773), meaning SWIM-RS underestimated daily ET variance by 23 percent at international sites. This variance dampening likely reflects the smoother ERA5-Land forcing relative to station-based GridMET: ERA5-Land captures synoptic-scale weather variability but can underrepresent local extremes — heat waves, frontal precipitation events, dry spells — that drive day-to-day ET swings at individual flux tower footprints. Despite this, ex-CONUS sites had lower median MAE (0.649 versus 0.707 mm d⁻¹) and lower RMSE (0.885 versus 0.958 mm d⁻¹) than CONUS sites, because the European and Southern Hemisphere sites in the cohort tended to have strong, predictable seasonal ET cycles well suited to the sigmoid crop coefficient framework.

Site-level win rates confirmed that SWIM-RS outperformed interpolated SSEBop at 67 percent of sites on RMSE and 74 percent on absolute bias. Against interpolated PT-JPL — the strongest individual comparator — SWIM-RS won 54 percent of sites on RMSE and 60 percent on MAE, but lost on absolute bias (42 percent), reflecting PT-JPL's inherently low site-level bias that calibration did not fully match. The ex-CONUS win rate against SSEBop was particularly strong at 75 percent on RMSE, indicating that the framework's international portability was not merely preserved but improved relative to the satellite benchmark, because SSEBop's cold bias was more severe at international sites where the local ETo climatology anchoring SSEBop's cold boundary condition was less well calibrated.

===== Discussion =====

=== What makes a good calibration target ===

Low bias relative to flux tower ET is a necessary but insufficient condition for a remote-sensing ET product to serve as an effective inverse-modeling target. A product can have near-zero mean bias against tower observations and still produce poor calibration outcomes if the information it provides to the parameter estimation algorithm is weak, inconsistent, or structurally incompatible with the forward model. For SWIM-RS, where the objective function is the sum of squared weighted residuals between simulated and observed ETf at individual overpass dates, four additional properties of the target time series matter as much as mean accuracy.

First, the target must have a stable temporal shape — a seasonal ETf trajectory that rises, peaks, and declines in a pattern the soil water balance can reproduce by adjusting its parameters. Noisy or erratic ETf sequences force the ensemble smoother to fit observation-level scatter rather than the underlying crop-water signal, inflating posterior parameter variance without improving the simulation.

Second, the target must have enough observations to define that shape. A daily soil water balance has eight free parameters per site; resolving them requires ETf samples distributed across the full growing season, including the critical shoulder periods of green-up and senescence where the sigmoid crop coefficient is most sensitive to its shape parameters. Sparse sampling concentrates calibration information in a few dates and leaves large portions of the seasonal trajectory unconstrained.

Third, the target must be consistent across years. PEST++ IES pools observations from the full simulation period into a single objective function, so year-to-year shifts in ETf level or timing that do not reflect actual changes in crop water use introduce systematic noise. If one sensor reports systematically higher ETf in some years than others for reasons unrelated to the field water balance, the ensemble smoother cannot distinguish sensor artifact from real interannual variability.

Fourth, the target must be compatible with the model's daily water-balance structure. SWIM-RS simulates ETf daily from the water balance and compares against observed satellite ETf only on overpass dates — non-overpass days receive zero weight in the PEST++ objective function. A calibration target whose temporal sampling is irregular, with long gaps followed by clusters, produces an uneven information density that can bias the calibrated trajectory toward dates with dense observations while leaving inter-observation periods poorly constrained. In practice, this means that an ETf product with nominally higher accuracy but irregular temporal coverage — for example, a thermal sensor with a long revisit and frequent cloud losses — can produce worse calibration outcomes than a product with slightly lower per-scene accuracy but consistent revisit, because the ensemble smoother needs evenly distributed information across the growing season to resolve the sigmoid crop coefficient and stress dynamics simultaneously.

These criteria explain why low bias alone is not sufficient for selecting among candidate ETf products, and why temporal consistency and target identifiability must be evaluated alongside mean accuracy when designing a calibration experiment.

=== Interpreting Example 4 Performance ===

The Example 4 benchmark also highlights several cautions in how network-scale skill should be interpreted. Mean R-squared was strongly negative for both SWIM-RS and SSEBop because a small number of catastrophic outlier sites dominated the lower tail of the distribution, making the median the more robust summary for this heterogeneous network. SWIM-RS site-level win rate also declined from 70% at the daily scale to 67% at the monthly scale, indicating that temporal aggregation narrows the process-model advantage relative to the satellite benchmark. In evergreen forests, both models showed positive bias of approximately 0.4 mm d⁻¹, suggesting that part of the apparent error in this class may reflect limitations in the flux tower reference rather than only model structure. Finally, because Example 4 calibrates parameters independently at each site, these results demonstrate strong performance within the calibrated network but do not yet establish that the inferred parameter distributions transfer directly to ungauged locations.
